Welcome

Welcome to "The Unseen Roots," a project exploring the inseparable link between the Palus people and the Palouse Prairie. When you look at the Palouse, you see rolling hills of wheat. My project is about what we *don't* seeâ€”the history and the people this agricultural landscape replaced.

I will contend that the historical removal and displacement of the Palus people directly contributed to the extensive agricultural transformation of the landscape, and that modern efforts to restore the native prairie must acknowledge and incorporate Indigenous knowledge and land management practices to be truly effective.

A Displaced People

Before settlers, the Palouse was a carefully managed environment. The Palus lived in semi-permanent villages, migrating in "seasonal rounds" to fish, hunt, and gather vital "first foods" like camas bulbs. This was active stewardship.

The transformation to farming was methodical. The Dawes Act of 1887 broke up communal Tribal lands into small, individual plots. "Surplus" land was opened to white homesteaders, replacing a shared ecosystem with a grid of private property.

An Altered Land

Today, less than 1% of the original Palouse Prairie remains. Organizations like the Palouse Conservation Environmental Institute (PCEI) are doing critical work to restore small patches of this native landscape.

However, this is where my contention comes in. Ecological restoration that ignores the region's human history is incomplete. To be truly effective, these efforts must incorporate Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK).

  • It's a Holistic Worldview: TEK is a worldview based on respect, reciprocity, and relationships, viewing the land not as a resource but as a relative to be honored.
  • It's Active Stewardship: This includes practices like cultural burning and re-prioritizing "first foods" like camas, which co-evolved with human harvesting.
  • It's Cultural Restoration: True restoration is also cultural, becoming a vehicle for revitalization by partnering with the descendants of the Palus people.

A Path Forward

The ecological state of the prairie and the human history of the Palus are not two separate issues; they are one. The landscape we see today was only possible after the removal of its original stewards.

Therefore, we cannot restore one without the other. The goal is not just to return the *plants*, but to restore the *relationship*. True restoration must be ecological, cultural, and social.
